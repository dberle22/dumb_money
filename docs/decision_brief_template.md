# Decision Brief Template

This template defines the standard format for a single-stock decision brief. It is produced at the end of a full three-lens evaluation (Value, Growth, Momentum) plus portfolio overlay. It is designed to be written once at decision time and revisited periodically to check whether the thesis remains intact.

The brief does not make the buy/sell decision. It surfaces the key evidence, the assumptions the thesis depends on, and the conditions that would change it.

---

## Page 1: The Thesis

```
[TICKER] — [Company Name]                                [Date]
Sector: [X]  |  Market Cap: $[X]B  |  Price: $[XX.XX]
Horizon: [Conviction Hold / Opportunistic Play]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VERDICT

[One sentence. Is this attractive right now and why, or is it not
and why? Verdict-first, not hedge-first.]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PRICE TARGET RANGE

  Bear      Base      Bull
  $[XX]     $[XX]     $[XX]

Base case
[2-3 sentences. What has to be true for this to play out as
expected? State the one or two assumptions the thesis stands or
falls on. Be specific — "margin expansion to 25%" is better than
"continued growth."]

Bull case
[What goes better than expected. A specific catalyst, re-rating,
or metric beat that would push toward the high end of the range.]

Bear case
[What breaks the thesis. Value trap? Priced for perfection?
Sector headwind? One short paragraph that honestly steelmans the
other side.]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LENS SUMMARY

  Value:      [Undervalued / Fairly Valued / Overvalued]
              [One sentence — the key reason.]

  Growth:     [High / Medium / Low quality]
              [One sentence — the key reason.]

  Momentum:   [Strong / Neutral / Weak]
              [One sentence — the key reason.]

Where lenses agree:
[One sentence. What all three (or two) are pointing toward.]

Where lenses conflict:
[One sentence. What is pulling in a different direction, or
"No material conflict" if aligned.]

Conviction vs. opportunistic framing:
[One short paragraph. How would this look as a 5-year compounder
vs. a 1-2 year trade? Which framing is more appropriate here and
why? What would change that assessment?]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PORTFOLIO FIT

  Proposed position size:          [X]% of portfolio
  Sector after adding this:        [X]% ([sector name])
  Correlated holdings:             [tickers, or "None significant"]
  If thesis is wrong, what else    [holdings that would also be
  in the portfolio gets hurt?      hurt by the same scenario]
```

## Notes on Use

**Price target range:** Generated from DCF scenarios. Base uses historical FCF CAGR and config defaults for discount rate and terminal growth. Bull and bear adjust the FCF growth assumption by approximately +5pp and -5pp respectively. These are mechanical starting points — the narrative next to each number is the important part.

**Revisit cadence:** The default thesis check is 90 days out. For conviction holds, extend to 180 days. The goal is not to second-guess every price move — it is to verify the one or two assumptions you named in the base case are still intact.

**The metrics table is a reading exercise.** Over time, pattern recognition across balance sheet and valuation metrics is how investment judgment improves. Use the table to build that habit, not just to fill in a form.

**Conviction vs. opportunistic framing:** This is not a mode selector. It is a stress test. Every stock gets both framings, and you decide which lens is the right one to hold the position under. A stock that only makes sense as a trade and not as a compounder requires more active management and tighter exit criteria.
